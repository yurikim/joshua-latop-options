The Selection Process & Logic

Choosing a laptop for engineering isn’t like buying a normal school laptop. Engineering software requires serious horsepower. We looked at dozens of options and filtered them down based on four major things:

1. The Brains: Processor (CPU)

Think of the processor as the laptop's speed limit. We looked for Intel Core Ultra 7 or AMD Ryzen 7 chips as the top preference but also considered Ultra 9 and Ryzen 9 -- it's overkill and can make the computer run hot but it was the best / only option sometimes.

2. The Muscle: Graphics Card (GPU)

When you are spinning a 3D model of a car engine or a bridge, the graphics card does all the heavy lifting. RTX 5060 / 5070 are "gaming" cards but are perfect for students because they offer massive raw power for a reasonable price. The RTX 1000 is a "workstation" card built for corporate offices. It isn't faster, but software companies (e.g. AutoCAD) guarantee it won't glitch—though you pay a high premium for that name. I looked into this quite a bit and I don't think the RTX 1000 is worth it because the other card is more flexible and very compatible. I left it in just in case it really matters to you, though.

3. Short-Term Memory: RAM

RAM lets you run multiple massive programs at once. 16GB is the college baseline, but 32GB is the sweet spot so the laptop doesn't lag when you have 50 Chrome tabs, a textbook PDF, and a 3D design program open together. Some laptops permanently glue (solder) the RAM inside. If it's glued, we must buy 32GB upfront -- that way it's future-proof. If it's upgradeable, we can buy 16GB now and cheaply add more later. Though you'll see most options have 32gb.

4. Portability: Weight & Size

A big laptop with a big screen is easier to read but is often heavier. A laptop that weighs over 5 pounds—plus a heavy charger brick—will make your shoulders ache. We aimed for laptops around 3 to 4.5 pounds to ensure it's easy to carry around all day. You have to decide how big / heavy you want to go.
